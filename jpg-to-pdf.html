Understanding Page Size Options

Auto (Match Image Size)

This option creates PDF pages that exactly match the dimensions of each image. Every page may be a different size, preserving the original aspect ratio and resolution of each photo. Perfect for photo albums where you want to maintain each image's unique dimensions without cropping or letterboxing.

Letter (8.5" x 11")

Standard North American paper size, ideal for documents that will be printed on US office printers. Letter size works well for scanned documents, business reports, and general-purpose PDFs. Use with "Contain" image fit to add white borders or "Cover" to fill the page.

Legal (8.5" x 14")

Longer than Letter size, Legal is perfect for documents with more vertical content, such as legal contracts, tall images, or multi-page scans that benefit from extra length without increasing width.

A4 (210mm x 297mm)

International standard paper size, used in most countries outside North America. A4 is ideal for global document sharing, academic papers, and international business documents. This is our recommended setting for most users.

A5 (148mm x 210mm)

Half the size of A4, A5 is perfect for booklets, small photo albums, flyers, and compact documents. Great for creating pocket-sized reference documents or small portfolios.

Photo (4" x 6")

Standard photo print size, ideal for creating photo albums, contact sheets, or printing images at photo labs. Each PDF page matches standard photo dimensions for easy printing.

Understanding Image Fit Options

Contain (Fit within page, maintain aspect ratio)

This option scales the image to fit entirely within the page while preserving the original aspect ratio (no distortion). If the image proportions don't match the page, white or black borders (letterboxing) are added. This is the recommended setting as it prevents cropping and distortion, ensuring your entire image is visible.

Cover (Fill page, may crop edges)

The image is scaled to completely fill the page, potentially cropping the edges if aspect ratios don't match. Use this for full-bleed photos where you want the image to touch all four edges of the page, perfect for photo albums and full-page images.

Stretch (Fill page exactly, may distort)

The image is stretched or squished to exactly match the page dimensions, regardless of aspect ratio. This may distort your images (make them look wider or taller). Only use this when exact page filling is more important than image proportions.

Original Size (No scaling)

The image is placed on the page at its original pixel dimensions. If the image is larger than the page, it will be cropped. If smaller, it will appear at original size with surrounding white space. Use for exact reproduction where scaling is undesirable.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Images Not Loading?

If images don't appear in the list, ensure they are valid JPG, JPEG, or PNG files. Very large images (50MB+) may take a moment to load. Check that your browser supports the File API (all modern browsers do).

PDF Too Large?

If the output PDF is too large, enable the "Compress images" option. This applies JPEG compression to reduce file size while maintaining good quality. For extreme size reduction, reduce image resolution in an image editor before converting.

Images Appear Distorted?

If images look stretched or squished, change the image fit option from "Stretch" to "Contain" or "Cover". "Contain" maintains aspect ratio and adds borders; "Cover" maintains aspect ratio but may crop edges.

White Borders Around Images?

White borders (letterboxing) appear when image aspect ratio doesn't match page aspect ratio with "Contain" fit. To eliminate borders, either:

  • Use "Cover" fit (may crop image edges)
  • Use "Auto" page size (matches each image's exact dimensions)
  • Crop images to match page aspect ratio before converting

Images Blurry in PDF?

If images look blurry, they may be low resolution. For best results, use images at least 150 DPI at final print size. Avoid enlarging small images significantly. If compression is enabled, try disabling it or reducing compression level.

Understanding Image Formats for PDF

JPG/JPEG (Joint Photographic Experts Group)

JPG is ideal for photographs, scanned documents, and images with gradients. It uses lossy compression (some data is discarded) to achieve smaller file sizes. JPG is the most common format for digital photos and scans.

PNG (Portable Network Graphics)

PNG uses lossless compression (no quality loss) and supports transparency. It's best for graphics, logos, screenshots, and images with sharp text or edges. Our converter supports PNG images as well.

When converting to PDF, both formats are embedded with their original quality preserved (unless compression is enabled). JPG images remain JPG in the PDF; PNG images remain PNG.
