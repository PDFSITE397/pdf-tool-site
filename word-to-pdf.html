Word to PDF conversion is the process of transforming Microsoft Word documents (DOCX or DOC format) into Portable Document Format (PDF) files. PDF is the gold standard for document sharing because it preserves formatting exactly as intended, works on any device or operating system, and cannot be easily edited without leaving traces. Understanding Quality Settings

Standard Quality

Standard quality creates PDFs optimized for screen viewing and general use. Images are downsampled to 96 DPI (standard screen resolution), fonts are subset to reduce file size, and color profiles are converted to RGB. This setting produces the smallest file sizes while maintaining excellent readability. Perfect for email attachments, document sharing, and web uploads where file size matters.

High Quality (Recommended)

High quality is our recommended setting for most documents. It preserves all formatting exactly as in the original Word document, maintains images at 150 DPI (sufficient for high-resolution screens and basic printing), and embeds all fonts used in the document. This setting provides the best balance between quality and file size, making it ideal for business reports, presentations, and documents that may be printed occasionally.

Print Quality

Print quality creates PDFs optimized for professional printing. Images are preserved at 300 DPI (standard print resolution), all fonts are fully embedded with complete character sets, color profiles are preserved (CMYK for commercial printing), and high-resolution vector graphics are maintained without compression. Use this setting for documents destined for professional printing, such as brochures, marketing materials, and publications.

Technical Details: How Our Word to PDF Converter Works

Our Word to PDF converter uses a sophisticated multi-stage conversion process:

  • DOCX Parsing: The DOCX file (which is actually a ZIP archive containing XML files) is parsed to extract all content including text, formatting, images, styles, and document structure.
  • Style Mapping: Word styles (Heading 1, Normal, etc.) are mapped to PDF structure elements for proper semantic tagging.
  • Font Processing: Font information is extracted, and either subset or fully embedded based on your settings.
  • Image Extraction and Embedding: Images are extracted from the Word document, optionally downsampled based on quality settings, and embedded in the PDF.
  • PDF Generation: Using PDF-lib, a new PDF document is created with pages sized according to Word page settings, content positioned according to Word layout, and all elements rendered appropriately.

Best Practices for Word to PDF Conversion

Prepare Your Word Document First

For best results, ensure your Word document is finalized before conversion. Check for consistent formatting, resolve any missing font warnings, verify that images are high enough resolution for your intended use, and ensure all hyperlinks work correctly.

Use Standard Fonts When Possible

While our converter embeds fonts, using standard fonts (Arial, Times New Roman, Calibri, Helvetica) ensures maximum compatibility. Unusual or custom fonts may increase file size significantly when embedded.

Optimize Images Before Inserting

High-resolution images (300+ DPI) greatly increase file size. For screen viewing, 96-150 DPI images are sufficient. Optimize images in an image editor before inserting into Word for better conversion results.

Review Converted PDF

Always review your converted PDF to ensure all elements converted correctly. Pay special attention to tables, complex formatting, footnotes, and any special characters or symbols.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Conversion Takes Too Long?

Large Word documents with many images or complex formatting may take a moment to process. This is normal as the converter analyzes all content. For very large documents (50+ pages with many images), allow extra processing time.

Fonts Look Different in PDF?

Ensure "Embed all fonts" is checked. If fonts still look different, the original Word document may be using fonts that cannot be embedded due to licensing restrictions. Consider converting text to outlines or using standard fonts.

Images Are Pixelated?

This occurs when source images have insufficient resolution. Use high quality setting and ensure original images are at least 150 DPI for screen viewing or 300 DPI for print. Re-insert higher resolution images into your Word document before converting.

Table Formatting Broken?

Complex nested tables may not convert perfectly. Simplify table structures, avoid merged cells when possible, and use simple borders. Review converted PDF and recreate complex tables manually if needed.

Hyperlinks Not Working?

Ensure hyperlinks in your Word document are properly formatted as clickable links (not just typed URLs). Our converter preserves standard hyperlinks formatted using Word's hyperlink feature.

Understanding DOCX vs DOC Formats

Our converter supports both modern DOCX (Office 2007 and later) and legacy DOC (Office 97-2003) formats. DOCX is an XML-based format that is more efficient, secure, and feature-rich.
